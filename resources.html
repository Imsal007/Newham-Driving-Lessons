"Show Me, Tell Me" Vehicle Safety Questions

Complete list of all current DVSA vehicle safety check questions with step-by-step answers and common mistakes to avoid.

Pro Tip

"Show me" questions require physical demonstration while explaining. "Tell me" questions are verbal only. Practice both types until they become second nature!

"Tell Me" Questions (Before Driving)

  • How do you check the brakes are working properly?
  • How do you ensure the head restraint is correctly adjusted?
  • How would you check the tyres have sufficient tread depth?
  • How do you check the lights are working?
  • How would you check the windscreen wipers and washers?
  • How do you check the horn is working?
  • How do you ensure there's no damage to the vehicle body?
  • How do you check the fuel level and other fluid levels?

Manoeuvres Mastery Guide

Step-by-step instructions for every manoeuvre you might be asked to perform during your test.

Parallel Park (Behind a Car on the Right)

  1. Check mirrors thoroughly and signal left to pull up alongside the vehicle (about 2 car lengths ahead)
  2. Position your car parallel to the parked vehicle, about 1 metre away
  3. Select reverse gear and check all around including blind spots
  4. Steer fully right as you reverse until you see the parked car's number plate in your left door mirror
  5. Straighten steering and continue reversing until your front door aligns with the rear bumper
  6. Steer fully left and continue reversing into the space, checking all around frequently
  7. Straighten wheels when parallel to the curb, leaving about 30cm from the curb
  8. Apply handbrake, select neutral, and switch off engine

Bay Park (Reverse In and Drive Out)

  1. Approach the bay slowly, positioning about 2 car lengths away from the bay lines
  2. Align your shoulder with the second line of the bay you're entering
  3. Check all mirrors and blind spots, steer fully towards the bay as you move forward
  4. Drive past the bay, stopping when your shoulders align with the far end
  5. Select reverse, check all around including blind spots
  6. Steer fully towards the bay and reverse slowly, adjusting as needed
  7. Use door mirrors to check you're within the lines
  8. Straighten wheels when fully in the bay, apply handbrake, select neutral

Pull Up on the Right and Reverse

  1. Check mirrors, signal right, and pull over to the right-hand side safely
  2. Position your car parallel to the curb, about 1 metre away
  3. Select reverse gear and check all around including blind spots
  4. Reverse slowly for about two car lengths, keeping close to the curb
  5. Check all around continuously, especially for approaching traffic
  6. Stop smoothly, apply handbrake, select neutral
  7. Check mirrors and blind spots thoroughly before pulling out

Emergency Stop Procedure

  1. When instructed, stop as quickly and safely as possible
  2. Press brake firmly and clutch down just before stopping
  3. Apply handbrake and select neutral once stopped
  4. When told to move off, prepare the car normally
  5. Check all around thoroughly including blind spots before moving off
  6. Move off safely when clear

Hazard Perception Mastery

Comprehensive guide to passing the hazard perception test. Learn what counts as a developing hazard and perfect your timing.

How It Works

  • 14 video clips showing real road scenarios
  • 15 developing hazards total (one clip has 2)
  • Maximum 5 points per hazard
  • Pass mark: 44 out of 75 for car drivers
  • Click when you spot a developing hazard

What Is a Developing Hazard?

A developing hazard is something that would cause you to take action – slowing down, stopping, or changing direction. It's actively developing and requires your response.

Perfect Your Timing

Click too early and you get zero points. Click too late and you get fewer points. The ideal window is when the hazard starts to develop but before it becomes critical.

Common Hazard Scenarios

Pedestrians

  • Pedestrians stepping off pavement
  • Children playing near the road
  • Elderly people crossing slowly
  • Pedestrians at uncontrolled crossings

Vehicles

  • Cars pulling out of junctions
  • Vehicles emerging from driveways
  • Buses pulling away from stops
  • Parked cars with doors opening

Cyclists & Motorcyclists

  • Cyclists emerging from blind spots
  • Motorcyclists filtering through traffic
  • Cyclists turning at junctions
  • Vulnerable road users in poor visibility

Weather & Conditions

  • Reduced visibility in rain/fog
  • Wet roads affecting braking distance
  • Ice and snow conditions
  • Glare from sun or headlights
Reference: DVSA Hazard Perception Guide

Test Day Preparation Guide

Complete test day guide to reduce anxiety and ensure success. Know exactly what to expect from arrival to completion.

Pre-Test Checklist

  • ✓ Provisional driving licence (photocard)
  • ✓ Theory test pass certificate
  • ✓ Test confirmation email/booking reference
  • ✓ Glasses/contacts if required
  • ✓ Comfortable shoes for driving
  • ✓ Arrive 10 minutes early

The Day Before

  • Get a good night's sleep (7-8 hours)
  • Eat normally, avoid heavy meals
  • Light practice only – don't overdo it
  • Avoid caffeine and alcohol
  • Prepare your outfit and documents
  • Plan your journey to the test centre

What Happens During the Test

Eyesight Check

Before driving, you'll read a number plate from 20.5 metres. If you wear glasses or contacts for driving, you must use them. Failure means immediate test termination.

Show Me, Tell Me Questions

One "tell me" question before driving, one "show me" question during driving. These assess your knowledge of vehicle safety.

The 40-Minute Driving Test

The test includes approximately 20 minutes of independent driving, various road types, junctions, roundabouts, and one manoeuvre.

Pass Mark

You can make up to 15 minor faults and still pass. However, even one serious or dangerous fault means failure.

Top 10 Common Test Failures

  1. Inadequate mirror checks before signalling/changing direction
  2. Poor observation at junctions
  3. Incorrect positioning when turning or at roundabouts
  4. Steering control issues
  5. Incorrect speed for conditions
  6. Poor clutch control (stalling, coasting)
  7. Inadequate observation when moving off
  8. Incorrect response to traffic signs/signals
  9. Poor judgement when overtaking
  10. Inaccurate manoeuvres
Managing Test Anxiety

Deep breathing exercises before the test can significantly reduce nerves. Remember: the examiner wants you to pass if you're safe. If you make a mistake, don't dwell on it – focus on the next task.

Reference: DVSA Guide to Learning to Drive
